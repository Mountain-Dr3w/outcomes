# Plain-language portfolio copy

## Problem

The current copy often names the structure of the work instead of its value. Phrases such as "seven-stage deployment pipeline," "strongest proof," and "validation in progress" are accurate, but they make the reader translate implementation detail into a reason to care. Several lines also compress caveats into headline space, which makes the portfolio sound defensive and machine-written.

## Reader

A hiring manager or design leader scanning quickly wants to know:

1. Who had a problem?
2. What was hard about it?
3. What did Drew change?
4. What became easier, clearer, faster, or safer?
5. What part did Drew personally own?

## Approaches considered

### Evidence-forward labels

Keep status and proof language prominent, but simplify each sentence. This preserves the current credibility system but still makes the reader think about portfolio methodology instead of the work.

### Punchy marketing headlines

Turn every project into a short, high-energy benefit statement. This scans quickly but creates the same "every line is a headline" problem and invites claims the available evidence cannot support.

### Selected: concrete stakes, quiet caveats

Lead with the user or team problem and the practical change. Put scope, status, and limitations in plain supporting language only where they affect interpretation. Let artifacts and narrative carry detail. This keeps the honesty of the evidence model without talking about the evidence model.

## Copy rules

- Start with the person, task, or decision affected—not the artifact built.
- Answer "so what?" in the same sentence or the next one.
- Prefer ordinary verbs: find, know, decide, start, fix, ship, wait, hand off.
- Use numbers only when they help the reader understand scale or impact.
- Do not use process nouns as benefits: pipeline, capability, operating picture, friction, signals, validation, evidence.
- Do not announce uncertainty in a headline. State limits quietly and specifically in the body.
- Avoid paired abstractions, em-dash flourishes, slogan fragments, and claims that sound optimized for a pitch deck.
- Keep labels literal. A label should tell the reader what follows, not make a claim about its importance.
- Preserve public-safe language and do not invent outcomes.

## Done when

Every public-facing string in the homepage, resume, case-study data, error state, and shared navigation passes this test: a reader can explain why the line matters without translating design or engineering jargon.
