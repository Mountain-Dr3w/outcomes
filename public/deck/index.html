J—01 Before

Modernized, but stuck
in one theater.

The task
"We can't expand to other AOCs. Fix it."
A previous team had already "modernized" tanker planning for one air operations center. I came on for what came next. The brief described a symptom, and finding the cause was my job.
The catch
Modernized, but nothing underneath was solved.
It moved planning off spreadsheets into a clean UI, but it was built around one center's local process instead of the outcome planners need. Nothing in it earned a second AOC's adoption.
The stakes
Every sortie depends on this plan.
A late or wrong plan means receivers don't get fuel, so sorties get cut and missions re-planned. A tool that can't travel leaves every other theater planning the hard way.
What I kept hearing

Other centers looked at it and didn't see their job in it, so they kept planning their own way.

J—02 The target · what good looks like

Two theaters running the same job,
and I went looking for the difference.

I split time between the 613th (Pacific) and 609th (Central) Air Operations Centers, two theaters running the same mission their own ways. I followed the plan end to end in each, separating what every tanker planner needs from what one office happened to do.

The reframe that set the direction

"Modernize the workflow" was the old goal, and the reason the tool couldn't travel. The real target was to design for the outcome every planner needs: a plan they can build quickly and trust, in any theater.

J—03 What I did · Framing

Design for what every planner needs,
not what one office does.

Trust
The plan checks itself.
A planner's core outcome is a plan they can trust. Conflicts validate as it's built, so a double-booking flags the moment it's made instead of after the phone rings. Every theater needs that, and none of it is local.
Clarity
Selecting a sortie shows what it touches.
Click any sortie and everything it connects to lights up: tankers, receivers, airspace. Managing those dependencies is the job in every theater, so the tool draws the ripple effects instead of asking planners to remember them.
Portability
Shaped by what planning produces.
The day at a glance, then filtered the way planners slice it. It's modeled on what planning produces rather than one center's naming and steps, and that's what let a second, third, and fourth AOC see their job in it.

J—05 What I did · Time and space, one canvas
Where I pushed back

Engineering pushed back hard on connected highlighting. It cut across data relationships system-wide, and the estimate was big.

I pushed for it because it serves the planner's core outcome: seeing what else moves when one thing moves. That's the job in every theater. Without it, the tool is only a cleaner form to fill out.

We kept the argument on what we'd watched planners do, not on taste, and scoped it to ship: highlight first, full dependency tracing later.

It shipped in v1.

F—01 Before

18–24 months from kickoff
to the warfighter.

The problem
No compliant, paved path to production.
The platform existed to get capability to the warfighter fast, and it couldn't. Standing a team up and reaching first deploy dragged on for months.
The waste
Year-long contracts that delivered nothing.
A 12-month proof-of-concept contract could start and end with nothing in front of a user. It was no one's fault, but the government bought a year for no return, and the vendor had nothing to re-compete on.
The trap
Two years of building, no feedback.
What reached the field after two years rarely got adopted, because it was built that whole time without real user feedback. Slow delivery quietly broke the feedback loop.
My slice · the target

Rise8 was brought in to pave the path. I owned onboarding, the front of it, where teams stalled for months. The target was to get a team from onboard to first deploy in days instead of months.

F—03 What I did · From anecdotes to data
The friction log

Every tenant pain point scored on four fields before it could enter a meeting: owning team · UX severity · breakage risk · rationale.

"A tenant complained about X" became "high-severity, breakage risk, owned by your team, and here's why." Meetings re-centered on what ranked highest instead of what was argued loudest.

The repeating patterns shared a root: internal habits had quietly become unspoken requirements for people on the outside.

DM—03 The common thread

I was handed a symptom both times.
The work was finding the outcome.

The symptom
The brief is never the problem.
"Modernize tanker planning." "We can't expand to other AOCs." "Onboarding is too slow." Each one is real, and each one is a symptom of something the brief doesn't name.
The outcome
Find what users need to achieve, then design only for that.
A plan any planner can trust and adopt. A path a team can get all the way through. I research until the real outcome is clear, then cut everything that isn't it.
The proof
The outcome shows up in how the work changes.
One AOC became four. Onboarding went from months to days. The result shows up in how people work now.

Stack

V—01 Before

An app that works on a laptop,
and never gets any further.

The user
Built something real, can't ship it.
A teacher tracking reading progress. A social worker replacing case-intake software from 2009. A clinic coordinator fixing a scheduling gap. All built with an AI assistant, all stuck at the same wall.
The bottleneck
The last mile assumes a DevOps background.
Putting an app online safely still assumes you know what a container is, what TLS is, how dependency vulnerabilities and environment variables work. That knowledge has nothing to do with whether the app is good.
The gap
Working locally is not the same as shippable.
The repo runs, but there's no Dockerfile, no env docs, and no honest read on whether shipping it would leak secrets. The jump from code to something deployable is bigger than it looks.
What I keep coming back to

Infrastructure is the last bottleneck between ordinary people and the impact their work could have. It isn't talent or ideas they're missing, it's the operational knowledge to get software online safely.

V—02 The target · the principle I designed to

Make the security review
something a non-coder can act on.

Here is my source code,
Run it on the cloud for me.
I do not care how. the platform-as-a-service ideal

That's the bar: you hand over your work and never see the machinery. The one thing that can't stay hidden is a real security risk, so the pipeline surfaces just that finding, in plain language, before anything goes live.

What that ruled out

Never raw scan output or YAML. Someone who doesn't code has to read a finding and act on it, and the tool should teach by writing good scaffolding rather than by lecturing.

V—03 What I did · the shape of it

A repo goes in, and a scanned,
running URL comes out.

Detect
Read the repo, figure out the framework.
Paste a GitHub URL and Velveteen detects the framework, then writes the files the project was missing: a Dockerfile, a .dockerignore, an env guide, and a standards file inferred from your own code.
Scan
Four scanners, before and after the build.
Gitleaks and Semgrep read the source before building. Trivy and Syft scan the built image after. Secrets, source issues, container vulnerabilities, and a software bill of materials, on every deploy.
Explain
Turn scanner output into a fix.
Raw scanner output is unreadable. Each blocking finding gets rewritten in plain English, with a prompt you can paste straight back into your coding assistant to fix it.
V—04 What I did · the front door

Everything you'd do across four pages,
on one calm screen.

ONBOARDING · REPO TO LAUNCH github connected · repo chosen · framework auto-detected · subdomain claimed · confident, not a DevOps dashboard
V—05 What I did · the gate
The decision that mattered

The gate can stop a deploy. The hard part was deciding what it stops for. Block on everything and people learn to ignore it, which is worse than no gate at all.

So I tuned it by hand. Secrets always block. Source issues block only at error severity. Container vulnerabilities block only at critical or high, with known framework noise downgraded to informational. The bill of materials never blocks.

And only the blocking findings get a written explanation, because those are the ones you have to act on. That kept the gate trustworthy and cheap enough to run on every deploy.

Dropped a two-model design for one.
About 15× cheaper. I pay for this myself.

L—01 How I build with AI

I didn't build Velveteen by hand.
I designed the process that built it.

I treat my own workflow like a product. First find the outcome: shipped, working software I'd put my name on. Then design the system that gets there every time, and cut the rest. The agents do the typing. The process is the part I designed.

Why bother with a process

Point an AI agent at a problem and it hands you code that looks finished. Half the time it never ran, skipped the security check, or made a call that wasn't its to make. Code that looks finished often isn't.

L—02 The process · three gates

Three gates the agents never skip.

Design first
A written plan before any code.
Nothing gets built until the intent is on disk. It's the same move as the case studies: the plan is where the design happens, the code is just the build. No agent skips ahead to typing.
Autonomous
Agents do the work, often several at once.
They plan, build, and fix their own mistakes in parallel. I review what they produce, the spec, the diff, and whatever's running, rather than watching every keystroke. It only stops for what's irreversible or genuinely mine to decide.
Verify
Nothing's done without proof it ran.
No "it works" without fresh output on screen. A typecheck gate and auto-formatting are wired straight into the loop, so the standards hold without me repeating them.
